    BASIC FEATURES OF THE DATA

    Before diving into the challenge, I take a quick look at the data.

    1. What's the size of the dataset? We have cell phone traces associated with two malls. There are 9,195 traces for Mall G and 50,294 traces from Mall S collected over a period of two weeks, from Jan 13th - Jan 26th, 2014.

    2. How many unique users are there? The Mall G and Mall S datasets have 5,959 and 33,797 unique users, respectively.

    3. What fraction of users visit the malls only once in the two-week period? Most users visit the malls just once. Mall G and Mall S received a total of 85% and 76.8% unique visits, respectively (see figure below).


     

    4. How much time do visitors spend at the mall? The median visit duration for Malls G and S are 1.1 hrs and 1.6 hrs, respectively. The average for Malls G and S is 1.4 hrs and 1.9 hrs. Because the mean is more strongly affected by outliers, we expect to see a right-skewed distribution of visit durations in both malls.

    5. How many events per trace are most typical? Between 2 and 10 events. In rare cases, traces have over 100 events.

    6. Are there any users that visit both Malls? The users at Mall G and Mall S are different and must be treated independently. Even though 418 user IDs are present in both datasets, their residency zip codes don't match, and therefore we can't assume a connection between them.

    7. Are there any potential biases in the data? The dataset for Mall G is small compared to that of Mall S, but we don't know whether this difference is due to sampling or whether Mall G is intrisically smaller. Also, we have traces for one cell phone company only. That would be a problem if our phone company is particularly popular or unpopular with the visitors of our malls. Also, not all traces are necesarily associated with events at the mall, but we assume that the dataset is dominated by true visits.
